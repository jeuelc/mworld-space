The invention of the telescope meant we could see lots more details of what the Solar System and the universe looks like. The first person to gaze up into space through a telescope was Galileo in the early 1600's. He discovered the brightest moons of Jupiter and the rings of Saturn. Galileo also recorded the planet Uranus, but he did not recognise it as a new planet. Telescopes now continue to grow in size, power and cost, with the Hubble Space Telescope costing 2.5 billion dollars.

In 1781, William Herschel found an object in the night sky and saw that it moved relative to the background stars, from night to night. Herschel realised it was a previously unknown planet, now called Uranus, further away and fainter than the ones people had known about for thousands of years.

In the early 19th century four more 'planets' were discovered, starting with the discovery of Ceres in 1801. These 'planets' were far smaller than the other previously known planets, and even with the best telescopes available they just looked like tiny dots of light.

These four small worlds orbited between Mars and Jupiter, and were actually the first asteroids to be discovered. By the end of the 19th century astronomers had found hundreds of asteroids!
