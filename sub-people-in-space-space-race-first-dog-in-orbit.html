First... Dog in Orbit

Launching a person into orbit is far harder than launching a radio transmitter. A person needs air, food and water. Getting a spacecraft back to Earth is also a challenge, as a spacecraft moving through the air at seven kilometres per second gets really, really hot.

Rather than launch a person into space, animals were sent first. In late 1957, Sputnik 2 carried Laika, a small dog, into orbit around the Earth. Laika only lived for a day or two before dying in her overheated capsule. That capsule was not designed to return safely to Earth, and Laika was always on a one-way trip to space.

The Soviet Union and United States flew dogs, monkeys, rabbits and mice in space to understand how to survive the demands of space travel.

Could people survive the crushing forces of being launched in a rocket? Could they survive being weightless and operate a spacecraft?
