Sometimes comets are so bright that everyone sees them. In 1910, the Earth passed through the tail of Halley's comet, which stretched across the entire night sky. Almost everyone around the world, both young and old, saw Halley's comet that year! Some comets are bright enough to see in daylight, at least if you know where to look.

Most comets are harder to find though. Many people search for comets using telescopes or binoculars. If you know what the night sky normally looks like, you can look for faint fuzzy blobs that look out of place. If the blob moves from hour to hour or from night to night with respect to the nearby stars, then it's possible that it is a comet wandering through the solar system!

How do comets get their names? Well, mostly they are named after the people who discover them. For example, Robert McNaught has discovered several comets, including Comet McNaught, which was seen around the world in 2008. One day, there might be a 'Comet You!'
